Footwear, function and the modern workplace

What really matters when fitting office shoes – for people who spend long days on their feet.

The reality of “office feet”

Many of the people Beth sees in clinic aren’t athletes – they’re office workers, retail staff, health workers and hospitality staff who stand or walk most of the day on hard, unforgiving floors.

The right footwear can soften that load. The wrong footwear can turn a manageable niggle into a persistent heel, forefoot or arch problem.

Key features Beth looks for

1. A sensible heel height

For most people, a slight heel is fine – and often more comfortable than being completely flat. But big jumps in heel height change how the foot loads.

  • Keep the heel height modest for everyday use.
  • If taller heels are required for dress codes, suggest limiting how long they’re worn.

2. Enough room through the forefoot

A narrow, pointed toe box might look smart, but it can compress toes together and increase pressure on the big toe joint and lesser toes.

  • Look for a rounded or gently squared toe box that lets the toes sit flat.
  • Ask patients to wiggle their toes in‑shoe – they should be able to move.

3. A sole that bends where the foot bends

Beth uses a simple “bend test” in clinic: hold the shoe at the heel and forefoot and gently bend. Ideally, it should bend roughly at the ball of the foot, not in the middle of the arch.

4. A stable heel counter

Gently squeeze the heel counter. If it crushes easily, it’s less likely to offer rearfoot stability – especially for people who use orthotics.

Talking about footwear without blame

Many people feel a little defensive about their shoes – especially if they’ve invested in a brand they like.

Beth often uses language like:

  • “This style has been doing a lot of work for you – let’s give your feet a break with something more supportive.”
  • “For long days on hard floors, your feet will often prefer…”
  • “We don’t need to throw everything out, but let’s find one or two pairs that your feet feel happier in.”
Inner West clinic tip: asking patients to bring in their three most‑worn shoes often reveals more than a footwear questionnaire alone.

When workplace policy is the barrier

Sometimes the challenge isn’t the patient’s preference – it’s a dress code or safety requirement. In those cases:

  • Clarify which elements are non‑negotiable (e.g. safety toe, colour, slip‑resistance).
  • Work within those boundaries to optimise fit, volume and support.
  • Consider a short note to HR or the workplace if medically necessary changes are reasonable.

Thoughtful footwear changes, combined with sensible load management, can make a big difference to workplace comfort – without asking people to give up feeling like themselves at work.
