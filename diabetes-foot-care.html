Diabetes foot care

Diabetes can affect circulation and nerves in the feet. Regular podiatry checks help catch problems early, reduce the risk of ulcers and keep you moving confidently.

Why feet matter in diabetes

Over time, diabetes can change the way blood flows to the feet and how well you can feel small injuries. This combination means that a small problem – like a blister, callus or crack in the skin – can turn into something more serious if it isn't noticed and treated early.

Regular foot checks with a podiatrist are about staying ahead of these changes, so you can keep doing the things you enjoy with as little disruption as possible.

What happens in a diabetes foot assessment?

Circulation & nerve checks

  • Checking pulses and skin temperature in your feet.
  • Simple tests of feeling using light touch or monofilament.
  • Looking for colour changes, swelling or changes in hair or nails.

Skin, nails & pressure areas

  • Checking for callus, corns and areas taking extra pressure.
  • Assessing nails for thickness, ingrowth or infection.
  • Looking between the toes for maceration or cracking.

Footwear & self-care

  • Reviewing your usual shoes and socks.
  • Discussing daily foot checks and skin care.
  • Planning how often you should have podiatry reviews.

Simple habits that help protect your feet

  • Check your feet daily for cuts, blisters, redness or swelling.
  • Moisturise dry skin, but avoid cream between the toes.
  • Keep nails trimmed straight across – ask for help if this is difficult.
  • Wear shoes or slippers around the house rather than going barefoot.
  • Let your GP or podiatrist know early if you notice any new breaks in the skin.

If you're on a chronic disease management plan, ask your GP whether podiatry is included – many patients are eligible for podiatry visits under these plans.
