Journal corner: recent research roundup

A quick, clinic-focused rundown of recent papers Beth has been reading on heel pain, running injuries and diabetic foot risk.

1. Plantar heel pain – loading, not just “stretch and rest”

A recent paper revisited exercise approaches for plantar heel pain, comparing simple stretching programs with progressive loading of the plantar fascia and calf complex.

So what for clinic?
Consider including progressive, tolerable loading (not just stretching) in management plans, and set expectations that improvement is gradual rather than overnight.

2. Running-related injuries – the value of “listening to niggles”

Another study followed recreational runners over time and highlighted that many overuse injuries had a “warning phase” of low-grade symptoms before becoming more limiting.

So what for clinic?
Encourage runners to treat early niggles as useful information, not something to push through indefinitely. Brief discussions about load modification and realistic timelines can prevent more significant setbacks.

3. Diabetic foot risk – small gaps, big consequences

Several recent pieces on diabetic foot complications emphasise that amputations often begin with small, preventable issues – minor trauma, unnoticed ulcers, or difficulties accessing timely care.

So what for clinic?
Reinforce the basics at every review: daily foot checks, footwear inspection, and knowing when to call early. Simple, repeated messaging often matters more than complex tools.

4. Communication and behaviour change

A recurring theme across many papers is that information alone rarely changes behaviour. How we frame advice, and how realistic it feels to the patient, plays a big role in what they actually do.

So what for clinic?
Aim for “one or two doable changes” per consult. Collaboratively choose something small the patient feels confident they can start this week.

Beth’s take-home: staying across every new paper isn’t realistic in a busy clinic, but having a feel for the direction of the evidence can help shape practical, patient-centred care.
